Perhaps most interesting: switching from monthly to hourly data completely flips which outcome the model predicts.

% ── 1. DATA ───────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Data}

Here's the central idea behind the paper (Li, Ren, and Bellos, 2026; hereafter ``the paper''): Bitcoin miners can act like sponges, soaking up excess renewable electricity that would otherwise go to waste. If true, mining doesn't just avoid harm. It actually nudges utilities to build more wind and solar.

I pulled three public EIA datasets to stress-test this:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{EIA Form 860}, sheet ``3\_1\_Generator (Operable)'': Plant Code, State, Maximum Capacity (MW), Energy Source 1.
\href{https://www.eia.gov/electricity/data/eia860/}{eia.gov/electricity/data/eia860/}
\item \textbf{EIA Form 923}, ``Page 1 Generation and Fuel Data'': Plant Id, State, Reported Fuel Type Code, monthly Net Generation (MWh).
\href{https://www.eia.gov/electricity/data/eia923/}{eia.gov/electricity/data/eia923/}
\item \textbf{EIA-930 Hourly Grid Monitor}: Hourly demand and generation by source for ERCOT ($n = 8{,}760$ hours, 2023). \href{https://www.eia.gov/electricity/gridmonitor/}{eia.gov/electricity/gridmonitor/}
\item \textbf{EPA eGRID} (2022, Table 1): ERCOT CO$_2$ emission factor, 0.386 MT/MWh fossil average.
\href{https://www.epa.gov/egrid}{epa.gov/egrid}
\end{itemize}

I sorted fuels by EIA code: SUN/WND $\to$ solar+wind, hydro/geo/biomass $\to$ other renewable, NUC $\to$ nuclear, everything else $\to$ fossil. Why Texas? It runs the largest grid in the country with both significant mining activity and fast-growing renewables.

\section{Analyses and Findings}

\subsection{Does mining coincide with more renewable capacity? (Proposition 1)}

\textbf{What I did:} Totaled maximum capacity by fuel type and year from Form 860 across Texas.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lrrrrr@{}}
\toprule
& 2019 & 2020 & 2021 & 2022 & 2023 \\
\midrule
Solar+Wind (GW) & 30.5 & 35.0 & 43.2 & 50.7 & 55.4 \\
Fossil (GW) & 98.6 & 97.6 & 100.5 & 102.4 & 103.9 \\
SW addition rate (MW/yr) & -- & 4,480 & 8,221 & 7,498 & 4,661 \\
Renewable share of gen & 18.8\% & 21.9\% & 24.2\% & 26.5\% & 27.4\% \\
CO$_2$ intensity (kg/MWh) & 280 & 268 & 260 & 253 & 252 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Texas capacity and generation summary (EIA 860 + 923).}
\end{table}

\textbf{Finding:} The numbers tell a clear story.
\begin{itemize}
\item Solar+wind surged 81\% (+24.9 GW). Fossil barely budged (+5.4\%).
\item CO$_2$ per MWh fell 10.2\%, even as total generation climbed 13.3\%.
\item Additions peaked in 2021--22 ($\sim$7,860 MW/yr), then cooled to 4,661 in 2023. Real growth, but front-loaded.
\item Compared TX against five wind-heavy states (IL, IA, OK, KS, MN). TX added $\sim$15,000 MW more than you'd expect. The catch? It was already outpacing them \textit{before} China's mining ban. Suggestive, not causal.
\item Halaburda and Yermack (2023) document the same dynamic: TX miners on wind power shut down more often yet earn more. They're buying dirt-cheap electricity during oversupply. Exactly the ``shock absorber'' role the paper envisions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Are demand and renewable supply actually uncorrelated? (\S3.1)}

\textbf{What I did:} Ran Pearson and Spearman correlations between monthly total generation (standing in for $\tilde{D}$) and renewable generation (standing in for $\tilde{S} \cdot k$). 60 months of data.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
Test & Value & $p$-value \\
\midrule
Pearson $r$ & 0.192 & 0.142 \\
Spearman $\rho$ & 0.180 & 0.168 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Demand-supply correlation (monthly, $n = 60$). Neither significant at 5\%.}
\end{table}

\textbf{Finding:} Demand and supply march to different drummers. Demand spikes in summer when everyone cranks the AC. Wind peaks in spring. These mismatched seasonal rhythms mean a high-demand month tells you almost nothing about renewable output. Li et al.\ argue this disconnect is precisely what makes mining valuable as flexible load. Now, weak correlation isn't the same as full independence. But glancing at the scatter plot (Fig.~3), there's no hidden pattern lurking either.

\subsection{Is the uniform distribution assumption for $\tilde{S}$ valid? (\S3.6.2)}

\textbf{What I did:} Computed capacity factors (CF $=$ actual output / max possible output) for every TX renewable generator. Then ran a KS test against a Uniform and fit a Beta for comparison.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
& Wind ($n = 949$) & Solar ($n = 386$) \\
\midrule
Mean CF & 0.324 & 0.211 \\
Std & 0.118 & 0.072 \\
Range & 0.01--0.56 & 0.01--0.33 \\
\% within 1 std of mean & 69\% & 72\% \\
KS stat vs.\ Uniform & 0.204 & 0.283 \\
KS $p$-value & $< 0.001$ & $< 0.001$ \\
\midrule
Fitted Beta$(\alpha, \beta)$ & Beta(2.60, 2.25) & Beta(2.58, 1.80) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Capacity factor distributions reject uniformity; Beta fits better.}
\end{table}

\textbf{Finding:} Here's where the data pushes back.
\begin{itemize}
\item Both distributions have a hump in the middle. Not flat. The KS test crushes the uniform assumption ($p < 0.001$).
\item A uniform overstates how often plants run near zero or near full blast.
\item Beta fits much better. Wind: Beta(2.60, 2.25). Solar: Beta(2.58, 1.80). One clear peak instead of a flat line.
\item What matters most: CFs still vary widely. The paper's big-picture conclusions likely survive under a Beta, but the precise thresholds in \S3.6.3 would shift.
\end{itemize}

% ── 3. CALIBRATION ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Calibration: Resolution Changes the Answer}

When I tried plugging real EIA numbers into the model's parameters for Texas 2023, something unexpected happened. The conclusion flipped depending on whether I used monthly or hourly data.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
& Monthly resolution & Hourly resolution \\
\midrule
$\bar{S}$ & $\approx 0.35$ & $\approx 0.57$ \\
$C/(c\bar{S})$ & $\approx 1.04$ & $\approx 0.64$ \\
Model prediction & Case 1 (no RE investment) & Case 2 (mining helps) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Different time scales, opposite predictions.}
\end{table}

\begin{itemize}
\item Monthly averages squash the true range of renewable output. They're a blurry photograph of a fast-moving target.
\item Hourly ERCOT data (EIA-930) paints a sharper picture: wind rockets from near-zero to 26.3 GW within days. During 1,143 hours (13\% of 2023), renewables supplied over half the grid.
\item Monthly $\bar{S} \approx 0.35$ $\rightarrow$ Case 1 (don't bother investing in renewables). Hourly $\bar{S} \approx 0.57$ $\rightarrow$ Case 2 (mining actually helps).
\item Same underlying data. Opposite takeaways. Any calibration needs hourly resolution at minimum.
\item Hu et al.\ (2015) flag this exact trap: measurement frequency can make or break renewable investment conclusions.
\end{itemize}


% ── 4. REGULATORY ENVIRONMENTS ────────────────────────
\section{Model Fit Across Regulatory Environments}

The paper assumes a regulator sets prices $p$ and $P(\cdot)$. That fits some market structures better than others. Borenstein and Bushnell (2015) outline three:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Regulated} (Southeast, Midwest). Strongest fit. A public utility commission sets rates and greenlights investments, which maps directly onto Eqs.\ 14--15. You could imagine the model's pricing mechanism working as a mining-specific interruptible tariff.

\item \textbf{Restructured} (ERCOT, PJM, CAISO). Trickier. Prices emerge from wholesale markets, not a regulator's desk. The intuition still works: renewables flood the grid, prices crater, miners gobble up the surplus. But nobody formally ``sets'' the price. Market power can warp those signals (Borenstein, Bushnell, and Wolak, 2002). And capacity markets introduce investment incentives the model doesn't capture (Joskow, 2008).

\item \textbf{Municipal}. Cleanest case. The utility is publicly owned and aims for public welfare. Regulator and utility are the same entity, so there's no tug-of-war between them. Proposition 1 should hold most strongly here.
\end{itemize}

One way to unify these: imagine a dial $\alpha \in [0,1]$ that controls how much the utility prioritizes public welfare versus profit. $\alpha = 1$ is a social planner. $\alpha = 0$ is pure profit-seeking. The paper's baseline sits near $\alpha = 1$.


% ── 5. LIMITATIONS ────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item Monthly data smooths over the rapid swings that trigger curtailment. Hourly helps (\S3), but even that's coarser than the near-instant adjustments the paper describes.
\item EIA doesn't track mining consumption on its own. I can spot broad patterns, but not what individual miners actually do.
\item Everything here is correlation, not causation. States friendly to mining might just happen to be great for renewables too. I ran a diff-in-diff to dig deeper, but the pre-trend didn't hold. Suggestive at best.
\item ERCOT is its own animal (energy-only market, no capacity payments). One grid doesn't speak for all grids.
\end{itemize}
